The Science of Today is the Technology of Tomorrow
Team Vertebrae is a student-run design team competing in a nationwide university-level design competition run by SEDS-CSA. Our mission is to design an experiment that will be run under reduced gravity conditions, providing valuable insight and information towards designing technology and exercises to further advance ease of travel into space by reducing the physical consequences of the trip

Who are we?

We are competing in a competition run by the SEDS-CSA created to promote interest in the space development industry. This is an exciting opportunity, and a one of a kind experience, as well as the first of its kind in Canada. Our goal is to design an experiment to be run in a reduced gravity simulation. We are being judged on the novelty of our idea, the ability to carry out the experiment, the ability to raise funding, as well as the quality, relevance, and importance of what we are trying to accomplish. We are working on research that will lead to the health benefits of anyone traveling outside our great big Earth. Altogether, we are a team made up of eight University of British Columbia students majoring in Engineering Physics, Electrical Engineering, Physics and Astronomy, Computer Science, and Economics. Our skills, ambition and passion foster our desire to work towards a better future, perhaps one that will make travel to space effectively less troublesome.

Context

When astronauts go out into space, they face spinal excitability. Simply put, they grow in height. The longer the travel time, the taller they grow. While for most of us this sounds quite appealing, it can have some serious implications on the human spine, especially once they return to Earth and their spines contract due to the gravity conditions. The main part of the human body associated with this phenomenon is contained within the Intervertebral Disk. There is a gel-like layer in between the spinal columns, which helps resist changes and stretches in the spine.

Our Experimental Design

We hope to use models or cadavers of parts of the spine to examine the stress exerted on the columns under reduced gravity conditions. The muscles exert forces onto the bones in order for postures to be maintained, and this is what we are investigating. The experiment would involve placing a spinal column model (or cadaver) at three different postures under reduced gravity conditions and measuring the stress through a strain gauge connected to a wheat stone bridge, in turn connected to a data acquisition medium. There will also be a control experiment done on Earth, under Earth’s gravity conditions.
